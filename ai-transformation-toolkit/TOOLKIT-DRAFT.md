# FRAMEWORK 1: The Diamond Org Structure

## What This Is

Every AI transformation plan assumes the current organizational hierarchy and bolts AI onto it. That's the wrong starting point. AI doesn't preserve the pyramid. It produces a diamond.

Leaders who plan for the diamond they're building toward — instead of the pyramid they're starting from — make fundamentally different decisions about hiring, role design, and investment.

## The Diamond

```
         ▲
        /|\
       / | \
      /  |  \        TOP: Shrinks and sharpens
     /   |   \       Constitutional governance, not information aggregation
    /    |    \      Small. Focused on principles, not decisions.
   /_____|_____\
  /      |      \
 /       |       \   MIDDLE: Expands significantly
/        |        \  Experienced professionals directing AI systems
\        |        /  Judgment-intensive oversight and synthesis work
 \       |       /   This is where investment goes.
  \______|______/
   \     |     /
    \    |    /      BASE: Compresses
     \   |   /       Routine analysis, processing, execution
      \  |  /        AI handles this. Fewer humans needed.
       \ | /
        \|/
         ▼
```

**Top layer:** Leadership stops being an information funnel and becomes a constitutional body. Their job is to define what "correct" looks like, set principles, and govern the system — not to aggregate reports and make operational decisions. This layer gets smaller and more focused.

**Middle layer:** This is the layer most transformation plans ignore. Experienced professionals who understand the domain deeply enough to direct AI systems, evaluate outputs, and catch what AI misses. This layer expands. It's also the hardest to hire for and the slowest to develop.

**Base layer:** Entry-level knowledge work that can be decomposed into verifiable tasks compresses. Not to zero — judgment and context still matter — but significantly. The people who remain at this layer are doing different work than they were before.

## The Gap Analysis Worksheet

Draw your current org chart. Then draw the diamond your organization will look like in three years if AI deployment continues on its current trajectory.

The gap between those two pictures is your transformation agenda.

**Three questions to pressure-test your current plan:**

1. Are you investing in the middle layer — the people who will direct AI systems — or mostly in tools and training?
2. Does your leadership team have a governance role defined, or are they still primarily decision-makers and information aggregators?
3. What happens to the base-layer roles in your organization? Do you have a plan, or are you hoping the question resolves itself?

# FRAMEWORK 2: The Leadership Alignment Audit

## What This Is

Most AI transformations fail before the first tool gets deployed. The failure happens in the leadership alignment meeting when three executives agree they're "all in on AI" while holding three completely different assumptions about what that means. Nobody surfaces the disagreement. Three months later, those assumptions collide and the initiative stalls. Everyone calls it change resistance. It isn't. It's misalignment that was never diagnosed.

## The Three Questions

Your leadership team needs to answer these — **specifically, not in the abstract**:

**Question 1: What work stays human-only?**
Not "strategic thinking" or "relationship management." Be specific. Name actual tasks, decisions, or outputs that your organization has decided will not be AI-augmented. Why? What's the principle behind that decision?

**Question 2: What work gets AI-augmented?**
Humans remain in the loop, but AI assists. For what tasks, specifically? At what point in the workflow does AI enter? Who decides whether AI output is good enough to use?

**Question 3: What work becomes AI-primary?**
AI handles it. Humans review outputs rather than producing them. Which tasks? What verification does the human perform? What's the escalation path when AI output is wrong?

## The 90-Minute Alignment Session

**Pre-work (1 week before):**
Each leader independently answers all three questions in writing. One page maximum. No discussion beforehand.

**Session structure:**

**0:00–0:15 — Ground rules.** The goal is to surface disagreement, not to reach quick consensus. Disagreement is data.

**0:15–0:45 — Read the room.** Each leader reads their answer to Question 1 aloud. Facilitator maps the answers on a whiteboard. No debate yet — just mapping. Repeat for Questions 2 and 3.

**0:45–1:15 — Work the gaps.** Identify the three biggest disagreements. For each: what's the underlying assumption that produces the different answer? Is it about risk tolerance, capability belief, organizational values, or something else? Name it.

**1:15–1:30 — Action items.** Which disagreements need a decision before implementation begins? Assign owners and deadlines. The ones that don't get decided here will decide themselves later — usually badly.

## Practitioner Notes

Don't skip this session because leadership seems aligned. Seeming aligned and being aligned are different things. The most dangerous alignment failures are the ones where everyone nodded without realizing they'd imagined different things.

If you can't get leadership in a room for 90 minutes before launching a transformation initiative, that's the most important data point you'll collect.

# FRAMEWORK 3: The Identity Change Arc

## What This Is

Most AI transformation plans treat people as a training problem. They're not. They're an identity problem.

When you tell a senior analyst that AI can do 70% of their work, you've told them that 70% of who they are professionally is about to become irrelevant. Of course they resist. The organizations that get adoption right don't lead with capability. They lead with leverage. The difference is night and day.

## The Reframe

**The wrong frame:** "AI can do 70% of your analytical work."

*Result:* Identity threat. The person's professional value was "I can process more information and make better decisions than most people." AI just changed that equation without offering a replacement.

**The right frame:** "Your expertise is now a direction system for AI. You decide where it looks, you evaluate what it finds, and you catch what it misses. That's higher leverage than doing the analysis yourself."

*Result:* The same capability, positioned as an upgrade rather than a threat. Same AI. Different outcome.

## The Six-Month Arc

**Months 1–2: Orientation.** People understand what AI can and can't do in their specific domain. They've run their own experiments. They have a personal opinion based on experience, not fear or enthusiasm.

**Months 3–4: Friction.** This is when people hit the ceiling of what AI does well and start encountering its failure modes. They get frustrated. Some get disillusioned. This is a necessary part of the arc — don't shortcut it.

**Months 5–6: Recalibration.** People who make it through the friction phase have developed genuine judgment about where AI adds value in their work and where it doesn't. They're starting to build workflows that use AI for what it's good at.

**Months 7–12: Integration.** AI is a natural part of how they work. They don't think about "using AI." They think about the problem. AI is a tool in the same way email is a tool.

## Stage Indicators

| Stage | What you see | What it means |
|-------|-------------|---------------|
| Orientation | "AI is impressive but I'm not sure where to use it" | Normal. They're calibrating. |
| Friction | "AI doesn't really work for my job" | Normal. They've found the limits. Don't intervene yet. |
| Recalibration | "It works for X but not Y" | They're building real judgment. |
| Integration | "How I work" (not "using AI") | Language shift is a real indicator. You're at month 7-8. |

## Practitioner Notes

The most common mistake: treating Months 3–4 friction as a failure of adoption. It's not. It's calibration. If everyone on your team thinks AI is great at month 4, they haven't learned where it fails. That knowledge is load-bearing.

**Three practitioner tips:**

1. **Name the arc upfront.** Tell people: "Here's what the next six months will feel like. Month 4 is going to be frustrating. That's not failure, that's learning. Here's what we're watching for."

2. **Celebrate useful skepticism.** When someone catches an AI error and flags it, that's the skill you want. Make it visible. Reward it explicitly.

3. **Track identity language.** When people stop saying "the AI" and start saying "my workflow" or "how I work," you're at month 7–8. That language shift is a real indicator.

# FRAMEWORK 4: Constitutional Principles vs. Compliance Rules

## What This Is

The best AI governance document I've seen was two pages. The worst was forty-seven. The forty-seven-page version covered every known scenario with specific rules. It was thorough, compliance-friendly, and completely useless in novel situations — which is exactly where AI governance gets tested.

A constitutional document scales with complexity. A compliance document breaks under it.

## Compliance Rules vs. Constitutional Principles

**Compliance rules look like this:**
- Employees may not use AI to draft external communications without manager approval.
- AI-generated content must be reviewed by a human before publication.
- Customer data may not be entered into AI tools not on the approved vendor list.

These rules cover known scenarios. They fail on novel ones. AI moves faster than any compliance framework can update.

**Constitutional principles look like this:**
- We don't ship anything we wouldn't put our name on, regardless of how it was produced.
- Our customers' data is treated with the same care we'd want our own data treated with.
- We use AI to do better work, not to do less work. The quality bar doesn't move.

These principles cover known and unknown scenarios. They get sharper with use. Every edge case is an opportunity to refine them.

## The 2-Page AI Constitution Template

**Section 1: Identity anchor (one paragraph)**
Who we are and what we stand for. The standard that AI doesn't change.

*Example:* "We are a company that produces work we'd be proud to put our name on. That standard doesn't change based on who or what produced it."

---

**Section 2: Decision rights (one page)**
Who can authorize AI use for what category of work. Three tiers:

| Tier | What it covers | Who authorizes |
|------|---------------|----------------|
| Individual discretion | Low-risk, reversible, internal use | Judgment of the individual |
| Manager approval | External output, customer-facing, medium stakes | Direct manager |
| Executive approval | High-stakes, regulatory exposure, precedent-setting | Named executive |

Be specific about what goes in each tier. "Medium stakes" is not specific enough.

---

**Section 3: Quality standards (3–5 bullet points)**
Not rules about process — standards about output. What does "good enough" mean? How do we evaluate AI-generated work against the same bar we'd apply to human-generated work?

---

**Section 4: Escalation path (one paragraph)**
When someone isn't sure whether something is authorized, here's what they do. One person, one decision point, fast response.

## The Test

For each principle: could every person in your organization use it to make a good autonomous decision about AI use in a situation the document has never contemplated?

If the answer is no, it's not a principle. It's a rule that hasn't been written yet.

# FRAMEWORK 5: The Transformation Timeline (and The Valley)

## What This Is

Most AI transformation plans show three phases: Pilot, Scale, Optimize. They don't show The Valley — the period between Pilot and Scale where results are inconsistent, organizational antibodies are active, and the people who approved the initiative start calling it "overhyped."

The Valley isn't a failure. It's a predictable phase. The organizations that navigate it successfully are the ones that named it before it arrived.

## The Timeline

**Months 1–2: Alignment.**
This takes longer than any plan expects. The Leadership Alignment Audit happens here. Do not start deploying tools until leadership can answer the three alignment questions with specificity.

**Months 3–5: The Valley.**
Tool adoption dashboards look fine. Actual capability development is rocky. Some team outputs are genuinely good. Others are worse than before AI. People are saying it's overhyped. Two things are true: AI is working for some tasks and not others, and people are still in Months 3–4 of the Identity Change Arc. This is not a sign that you chose the wrong tools. It's a sign that you're in The Valley.

**Months 6–12: Integration.**
Things start working. Patterns emerge. The people who got through the friction phase are getting good at AI-augmented work. Their outputs are visibly better than pre-transformation outputs. The organizational antibodies are weaker because there are now internal examples of what good looks like.

**Months 12+: Metrics become meaningful.**
Before this point, measuring outcomes is premature. The capability is still being built. After this point, you can start asking whether AI is actually improving business outcomes — because now you have a stable enough baseline to compare against.

## The Inoculation Script

Before The Valley arrives, say this to your team and your sponsors:

> "There's a phase in every AI transformation — usually around months three through five — where results are inconsistent and the initiative feels shaky. The tools that worked well in the pilot feel less reliable at scale. People are frustrated. Some are saying we should go back to how things were. **This is normal. It doesn't mean the transformation is failing. It means we're building capability, and capability development is uneven.** Here's what we're going to see, and here's how we'll know we're through it."

When The Valley arrives on schedule, and you've told people it was coming, your credibility increases instead of decreasing.

## The Diagnostic Indicator

**The single indicator that distinguishes normal Valley from a real problem:**

Are individuals showing improvement in their AI-augmented work, even if team-level results are uneven?

- Individual improvement during The Valley → on track
- No individual improvement → investigate

If nobody on your team is getting individually better at AI-augmented work by month 5, the problem isn't The Valley. The problem is something else — and it needs a different response.

# FRAMEWORK 6: What to Measure (The Anti-Metrics)

## What This Is

Most organizations track AI tool usage rates. High usage equals successful adoption. This metric is wrong.

Some of the worst AI use looks great on usage dashboards. People generating volumes of mediocre output because the tool is easy to use, not because they're using it effectively. Measuring effort instead of capability development is the most common measurement mistake in AI transformation.

## The Four Metrics That Matter

### 1. Iteration Quality
Are outputs on the third attempt better than outputs on the first attempt? Improving trajectory matters more than absolute quality at any given moment.

*How to track it:* Have managers score work product quality on a simple 1–5 scale weekly. Don't benchmark against a standard — benchmark against prior week. Look for direction, not level.

---

### 2. Error Detection Rate
Are people catching AI mistakes before they reach stakeholders? This skill improves with practice, but only if people are actually using their judgment to evaluate AI output rather than passing it through uncritically.

*How to track it:* Ask teams to log AI errors they caught before submission. Don't make this punitive. The goal is to make error detection visible and valued, not to penalize people for AI mistakes.

---

### 3. Decision Velocity
Time from information availability to decision implementation. AI should move this number. If AI is in use but decision velocity isn't improving, something in the workflow is broken.

*How to track it:* Pick three to five decision types your team makes regularly. Time them from trigger to implementation for one month. Repeat at month six and month twelve.

---

### 4. Escalation Frequency
How often do autonomous teams need leadership input for decisions within their defined scope? This should decrease over time as constitutional interpretation matures.

*How to track it:* Count leadership escalations per team per week. Look for a downward trend starting around month six.

## What to Stop Measuring

**Usage rates.** Measures effort, not capability. Easy to game. No relationship to outcomes.

**Prompt counts.** Same problem.

**Time saved estimates.** People are notoriously bad at estimating this accurately, and it doesn't tell you whether the time saved was invested in better work.

**Satisfaction surveys at month two.** Too early. People haven't been through The Valley yet. Satisfaction will drop before it rises. Measure at month twelve.

# BONUS: Prompt Kits

_Two prompt kits included with the toolkit. Full prompt text included in separate PDF._

## Kit 1: The Harness Is the Story

**Prompt 1: Verifiability Audit**
Maps your work to the harness frontier — what's ready for AI delegation today, what needs verification infrastructure first, and what stays human.

**Prompt 2: Harness Simulator (Planner-Worker-Judge)**
Takes a complex task and works through it using P-W-J architecture. Decompose, execute, evaluate, revise. Substantially better output than single-shot prompting.

**Prompt 3: Evaluation Meta-Skills Map**
Identifies the specific evaluation competencies in your domain that become more valuable as AI handles more execution work.

---

## Kit 2: AI Shows Us the Job Was Never the Job

**Prompt 1: Coordination Tax Audit**
Decomposes your actual work week into coordination overhead, verifiable execution, and genuine judgment work — with exact percentages.

**Prompt 2: Role Vulnerability Assessment**
Maps your role's structural exposure — not which tasks AI can do, but which tasks will stop existing when the coordination layer collapses.

**Prompt 3: Residual Skills Inventory**
Identifies and stress-tests the durable capabilities you already have, and exposes the gaps you need to close.

**Prompt 4: 90-Day Repositioning Plan**
Converts your audit results into a concrete action plan to shift toward the durable residual. Week-by-week, specific, executable.
